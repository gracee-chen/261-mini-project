\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Image classification plays a central role in medical AI, enabling automated recognition of pathological patterns in radiology, histology, and dermatology. Over the past decade, convolutional networks~\citep{he2016deep,tan2019efficientnet,liu2022convnet}, vision transformers~\citep{dosovitskiy2020image}, and self-supervised pre-training methods~\citep{fang2023eva02} have each pushed the state of the art, while feature-based classifiers~\citep{donahue2014decaf} remain relevant when compute budgets are tight. Despite this progress, medical imaging datasets are typically medium-scale and class-imbalanced, and head-to-head comparisons of these paradigms under identical evaluation protocols remain scarce.

We address this gap using Caltech-101~\citep{fei2004learning} as a controlled testbed: its 9{,}000 images, 102 categories, and pronounced class imbalance mirror challenges common in clinical datasets. We fine-tune five deep architectures (\texttt{ResNet-50}, \texttt{EfficientNet-B2}, \texttt{ViT-B/16}, \texttt{ConvNeXt-Tiny}, \texttt{EVA-02-Small}) and train an \texttt{SVM} on frozen \texttt{ResNet-18} features, all sharing the same stratified data split, augmentation pipeline, and evaluation metrics. Beyond model comparison, we ablate optimizer choice and data augmentation to isolate their individual effects. The resulting analysis and open-source codebase aim to guide practitioners selecting architectures for similar medium-scale recognition tasks.
